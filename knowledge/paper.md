# Promptotyping. Translating Research Data into Research Artefacts with Context and Agentic Engineering

Christopher Pollin (Digital Humanities Craft OG, Graz)

> **Status.** Working draft (2026-07-23). Bracketed markers `[...]` flag facts to verify and open decisions; the steering knowledge lives in `paper-writing.md`. British spelling throughout.

## Abstract

Digital research data is accessible only through software, and generic tools remain beneath the data model a project has built; whether a research instrument matches the model has so far depended on development capacity. This paper introduces Promptotyping, an iterative, document-driven context engineering method within agentic engineering, through which individual researchers and small teams can translate their structured research data into functional research artefacts, from interactive interfaces to pipelines and proto-editions. The method maintains requirements, data descriptions, and design decisions as versioned Markdown documents, the Promptotyping Documents, from which LLM-based coding agents derive the artefact; the documents are the primary artefact, and the prototype is a by-product that can be discarded and regenerated. The paper's theoretical core is a doubling of the word translation. The translation problem between domain scholars and programmers, documented in the field for two decades, meets LLMs operating as translation mechanisms, and both translations meet in the documents, which function as boundary objects. Human expertise concentrates on modelling, specifying, and verifying, the work that carries scholarly responsibility. The artefacts are self-contained static web tools classified by the epistemic function they serve (Verification, Exploration, Edition, Capture, Audit). Evidence comes from documented projects of roughly the past two years, each traceable in its public repository and quantitatively verified against it, and from teaching cases that test the method's transferability to non-hybrid scholars. The contribution is methodological and conceptual; the discussion names the limits, among them the single-practitioner evidence base, the missing control condition, and the confounding of method effects with model capability.

## 1. Introduction

Digital research data is accessible only through software, and the affordances of that software determine which questions can practically be asked. A text editor opens a CSV file for reading and searching. A spreadsheet adds sorting, filtering, and aggregation. Gephi[^1] renders relational data as an explorable network. Even the most specialised of these tools remains generic relative to the data model. Gephi knows nodes and edges, while the relation types and uncertainties that the scholar has modelled into the data lie outside its vocabulary.

A data model is built to answer the questions of its project. Historical financial records, the source type my dissertation models and operationalises (Pollin 2025b), show how the questions shape the model. A social historian and an economic historian may study the same series of account books and model it differently. For prosopographic questions the model records who buys what from whom, and when. For questions about price development it records commodities, quantities, and prices over time. The same source yields two models, and each calls for a different instrument. The two perspectives are documented in the dissertation as worked-out personas with their sources and user stories, the social historian on the Wheaton Day Book, the economic historian on the Jahrrechnungen der Stadt Basel (Pollin 2025b). To ask such questions through a generic tool, the scholar must first reduce the model to what the tool can represent, and the reduction discards exactly the distinctions the questions depend on. The dissertation records the same limit on the tool side. The generalised dashboard of its publication platform served broad overviews and flattened the context-specific detail that social and economic historians require, and Promptotyping originated as a response to this constraint (Section 2.6). Instruments that preserve the model must be built, and building them requires development capacity. An individual researcher can model data with great care and still have no instrument that matches the model.

Data in the humanities is interpretatively modelled and operationalised, made by people for people, and no generative system replaces this foundation. The method presented here presupposes it, because well-modelled data is precisely what its context layer curates (Section 2.3).

What limits the building of instruments is development capacity, and behind the capacity problem lies a translation problem between domain scholars and programmers that the field has documented for two decades. Edmond (2005) described the professional intermediary who mediates between scholarly requirements and technical implementation. Siemens (2009) analysed the team structures that digital humanities projects build to hold both competences together. Kemman (2021) reconstructed the trading zones in which historians and computational experts negotiate shared meaning. The Research Software Engineering movement has institutionalised the intermediary as a recognised profession (Baxter et al. 2012; Cohen et al. 2021). Each of these answers installs a human translator. I infer, from the field's own accounts of intermediaries and RSE capacity as scarce, of teams as bound to project funding, and of hybrid scholar-developers as a small population, that none of them reaches the individual researcher or the small project as a matter of course.

This paper introduces Promptotyping, an iterative, document-driven context engineering method situated within agentic engineering. With the method, individual researchers and small teams can translate their own structured research data into functional research artefacts, prototypes for exploring and analysing the data, a class of research acts previously bound to structures with dedicated development capacity. LLMs operate as translation mechanisms. They translate natural languages into one another, requirements into code, and research data into research artefacts. Section 2.4 develops this characterisation and connects it to the translation problem above. Both translations, the human one and the machine one, meet in the same artefact, the documents.

The results are prototypes, functional and verifiable research instruments with a boundary. Where a project outgrows the prototype, it enters software development and the domain of Research Software Engineering (Section 4.3). The method presupposes structured research data, and its effectiveness scales with the quality of the data's modelling (Section 2.3). The contribution itself is methodological and conceptual. The paper offers no comparative measurements of development time or cost, and the discussion names the missing control condition as a limit.

The capability behind this claim predates its tooling. In June 2023, in a workshop series on applied generative AI in the digital humanities, I demonstrated LLM-supported visualisation of research data, biographical data of translators who fled into exile, with Python and Jupyter notebooks (Pollin 2024). That demonstration required a hybrid practitioner who drove every step by hand, prompt by prompt. Between then and late 2025, frontier-model[^2] coding capability converged with agentic harnesses such as Claude Code[^3], tools in which an LLM agent navigates a repository, executes scripts, runs tests, and iterates on its own output (Section 2.5). This convergence moves the unit of work from the prompt to the document. Against a single prompt, structured context is helpful; against an agent that works for extended periods without supervision, documents are the governing specification[^4], the only stable instance of intent in a process whose intermediate states no one re-reads in practice. Promptotyping names the working mode under this changed condition.

The argument proceeds from the humanities. Positionally, the evidence base is my documented practice as a Research Software Engineer for digital humanities projects. Substantively, humanities research data has a specific difficulty profile. It is heterogeneous, rarely schema-regular, interpretatively modelled, and semantically dense, and uncertainty is a first-class property of the data. For historical financial records, my dissertation describes this profile as the interplay of human agency, context sensitivity, multiperspectivity, and uncertainty (Pollin 2025b). A method that holds up against this profile plausibly transfers to fields whose data is more regular. I offer this as an inference in the favourable direction, developed in Section 6.5, and the evidence base of this paper remains the humanities.

This paper is the canonical definition of the method. Promptotyping was developed inductively through practice. Its precursors are the definition in my dissertation (Pollin 2025b), the initial method description in a blog publication (Pollin 2025d), and a public blog contribution on its positioning (Pollin 2026). Section 3.1 states what the consolidation entails. The method, in turn, presupposes the scholar's profession. Modelling, specifying, and verifying remain the researcher's work throughout, and the method does not confer the experience and judgement of the Research Software Engineering profession on its users. What it changes is the reach of a specifying and verifying scholar within the prototype boundary.

Section 2 establishes the ground on which the claim rests, why exploration is a research act, how LLMs relate to research data, and why the characterisation of LLMs as translation mechanisms answers the field's translation problem. The method itself, its phases, its documents, and a worked example follow in Section 3. What the method yields, and where its boundary to Research Software Engineering runs, is the subject of Section 4. The claim is then put to the test, in Section 5 against a selection of documented projects and experiments from roughly the past two years, each traceable in its public repository, and the teaching cases, in Section 6 against its own limits, from verification to reproducibility. Section 7 returns to the two translations and states what they answer.

## 2. The Epistemic Frame

Everything this section assembles serves one argument, the doubling of the word translation that Section 2.4 develops. The translation problem between domain scholars and programmers, documented in the field for two decades, and the language model that operates as a translation mechanism meet in the Promptotyping Documents. Each subsection around 2.4 establishes one of its premises. Exploration is a research act that lacks its instrument, tools carry epistemic weight and stay disposable at once, well-modelled data is the ground the generation stands on, and the method's genealogy shows the doubling growing out of humanities practice.

### 2.1 Exploration as Research

The information visualisation discourse of the digital humanities has established that exploration is research. Whitelaw (2015) contrasted the withholding search box with generous interfaces that show the collection, invite browsing, and reward the visitor who does not yet know what to ask. Hinrichs, Forlini, and Moynihan (2019) argued that visualisation in the humanities is a research process in its own right, and offered the sandcastle as its emblem, a construction whose value lies in the building and whose impermanence belongs to the genre. Windhager et al. (2019) surveyed exploratory visualisation for cultural heritage collections and documented both the richness of the design space and how few projects can afford to enter it. The discourse thus knows what exploratory interfaces are good for, and the field can rarely build them.

Drucker's intervention serves this paper as a touchstone. In her programmatic essay and in Graphesis (Drucker 2011; Drucker 2014), she argued that the humanities deal in capta, constructed and situated observations rather than given data, and that inherited visual conventions, the bar chart, the timeline, the map, smuggle positivist assumptions into humanistic material. This critique applies with force to LLM-generated visualisations, because language models have learned exactly the standard conventions Drucker criticises from the aggregate of the web and reproduce them by default. A generative method that ignored this would automate the problem. Promptotyping's answer is structural. Because every artefact derives from explicit documents, visualisation decisions are lifted out of the model's defaults and into reasoned, versioned specifications that the scholar writes and can defend. A design document can state that a timeline must represent dating uncertainty as an interval rather than a point, that a network graph must not imply directionality the sources do not warrant, or that missing values must remain visible as missing. Whether a given project meets Drucker's standard is then an inspectable question about its documents. Section 3.4 shows this mechanism in a concrete case, and Section 6.4 returns to its limits.

### 2.2 Tools as Epistemic Artefacts, and the Limits of Tool Enthusiasm

The digital humanities have long argued that building is a way of knowing. Galey and Ruecker (2010) proposed that a prototype can embody an argument, that design decisions assert claims about the material they organise, and that a tool can therefore be read and criticised the way a monograph is. The INKE research programme extended this into a typology of prototypes as experiment, development, and provocation (Ruecker 2015). If the prototype argues, then producing prototypes is a research act in its own right.

The same discourse has developed the corrective. Digital tool criticism (Koolen, van Gorp, and van Ossenbruggen 2019; van Es, Wieringa, and Schäfer 2018) demands that scholars reflect on what a tool does to the material, which assumptions it embeds, and whether it suits the research question at hand. This paper adds a second corrective and applies it to itself, the rejection of tool positivism, the assumption that research problems reduce to tooling problems. In the method presented here, the tool is a derived and disposable artefact. The epistemic centre is the modelled research data together with the documents that describe, specify, and justify what is to be built from it. A promptotype can be discarded and regenerated. The modelling and the documents persist, and their quality is what the quality of every generated artefact tracks (Section 5.5). The tool matters because it makes the data addressable; the scholarship lies in the data and its modelling. Whether disposability and durability can hold together in one method is the tension Section 6.1 resolves.

### 2.3 LLMs and Research Data

I use research data in Borgman's sense, as representations of observations, objects, or other entities used as evidence of phenomena for the purposes of research or scholarship (Borgman 2015). In the humanities this genus takes a specific form. My dissertation adopts Schöch's characterisation of humanities data as a selectively constructed, machine-actionable abstraction that represents some aspects of a given object of humanistic inquiry (Schöch 2013; Pollin 2025b), an abstraction whose construction carries the difficulty profile Section 1 described.

If the modelled data is the epistemic centre, the relation between data quality and LLM-based generation needs to be stated precisely, and it has two dimensions that pull in opposite directions.

The first dimension is the semantic explicitness of the source data. Simply structured formats such as CSV and JSON are the minimum condition. Standard-conformant formats such as TEI-XML, LIDO, CMIF, or PAGE XML carry more meaning. Ontology-based data in RDF is the ideal case of modelling, because there the meaning of the data is itself machine-readable, with classes, properties, and constraints that encode domain semantics explicitly. Standards and ontologies provide the shared vocabulary that bridges between the researcher's intent and generated code. This is the FAIR programme read from the context side; data curated for interoperability and reuse under the FAIR principles (Wilkinson et al. 2016) carries its meaning in exactly the explicit, standardised form that generation profits from. The more semantics the data carries, the less the method's context layer must supply.

The second dimension is the token economy of the working context. Here the ordering reverses. RDF serialisations and richly encoded TEI are expensive in tokens; CSV and JSON are cheap. Long-context degradation is empirically documented. LLM performance declines non-uniformly as input length grows, even on simple retrieval tasks (Hong et al. 2025). A method that passed full semantic corpora through the context window on every step would defeat itself.

Promptotyping resolves this tension with a simple arrangement. The model reads about the data and writes code that reads the data. The semantically rich source data remains the source of truth. What enters the model's context is a distilled, token-efficient description of it, produced in the Distillation phase (Section 3.2), in larger projects partly by deterministic scripts that traverse the full corpus and render compact structural descriptions (Section 3.3). And the artefact itself is produced by generated code, parsers, transformations, and rendering logic that process the data deterministically and repeatably. The full data never passes through the context window. What passes through are the data description, the user stories, the design decisions, and the feedback. Where the documents that carry this description sit on the spectrum of explicitness, and why native processability does not lift the token budget, Section 3.3 works out.

This arrangement continues a research programme the humanities have invested in for two decades. The Semantic Web's founding vision was agents operating over semantically described data (Berners-Lee, Hendler, and Lassila 2001). That vision presupposed the AI paradigm of its time, explicitly defined rules and formal inference, and it did not materialise on that path. Berners-Lee himself has recently revisited the agent idea and locates its possible realisation in LLM-based systems (Berners-Lee 2025) [exact passage to verify]. The claim this paper needs is narrower than the vision. LLM agents do not perform formal inference, and nothing here suggests they replace reasoners or SPARQL. They profit from explicit semantics as context. Data whose modelling encodes domain meaning in standard vocabularies is high-quality context by construction, and the community that spent twenty years building ontologies, authority files, and semantic standards finds that this investment pays out in the LLM era through a different mechanism than the one envisioned. What pays out is the explication work. The semantics, once made explicit, outlasts the format that first carried it, and only that format as sole carrier becomes dispensable. My own trajectory instantiates this connection. The dissertation produced RDF data of historical account books precisely so that they could be explored, and the exploration is what the method described here finally delivers (Section 2.6).

### 2.4 The Translation Problem and Documents as Boundary Objects

Section 1 introduced the translation problem between domain scholars and programmers and the field's answers to it, the intermediary, the team, the trading zone, and the Research Software Engineering profession. The institutionalisation of RSE sharpens the problem as much as it answers it. RSE capacity concentrates at well-resourced institutions, and I take the long tail of small projects and individual researchers to remain underserved, the same inference Section 1 draws from the field's accounts of scarce capacity.

Promptotyping Documents intervene in this problem as boundary objects in the sense of Star and Griesemer (1989), objects plastic enough to serve the needs of different communities and robust enough to maintain identity across them. A data document is readable by the scholar, who can verify that the description of the sources is right, and actionable for an agentic tool, which derives parsing and interface logic from it. A requirements document phrased as user stories is at once a scholarly statement about research practice and a specification. The method thereby relocates the site of interdisciplinary translation. What Kemman describes as negotiation in conversation between two people becomes a versioned, inspectable artefact; the trading zone acquires a written constitution. This is itself a claim about how interdisciplinary knowledge can be stabilised, and it holds independently of any generative tooling. A Promptotyping document set would improve communication with a human Research Software Engineer too, a point Section 6.5 returns to. In the generative setting the documents carry a second load. They are also the source from which the implementation is derived.

That second load is what gives the word translation its mechanistic sense, and the doubling is the paper's theoretical core. LLMs operate as translation mechanisms. They translate natural languages into one another, requirements into code, and, in the arrangement described here, research data and research context into research artefacts [the characterisation of LLMs as translation mechanisms owes to a conversation with a colleague; attribution to be clarified]. Promptotyping, read through this lens, organises a chain of such translations. Scholar-Centred Design translates research practice into user stories; Distillation translates data into token-efficient descriptions; the agent translates documents into code; the code translates data into a visible, addressable form. What the method adds to the raw capability of the model is that every step of this chain leaves a versioned, inspectable trace, and that the documents stand at each junction as the stable objects both sides can read. The two senses of translation meet exactly here. The interdisciplinary translation problem of Edmond, Siemens, and Kemman is answered by making the machine translation's inputs, the documents, serve simultaneously as the medium of the human translation. Seen from the modelling tradition, the step the machine takes over has a name, formalisation; the path from a natural-language description of a domain to a running system used to pass through human formalisation work, and Section 3.3 grounds this in the vocabulary of conceptual models.

### 2.5 Position in the AI Discourse

Five terms locate the method in the current discourse, condensed here to what the argument needs.

_Context engineering_ is the systematic design and management of the contextual information provided to LLMs at inference time (Mei et al. 2025). The shift from prompt engineering to context engineering reflects that production-grade LLM applications require information architecture rather than individual prompt optimisation. Promptotyping is a domain-specific instance, optimised for mapping research data and research context onto functional artefacts.

_Vibe Coding_ (Karpathy, February 2025) names the practice of generating code through natural language and accepting it without thorough review; empirical studies document its speed and its reliability problems (Sarkar and Drosos 2025; Fawzy et al. 2025). Promptotyping shares the premise that LLMs can generate functional code from natural language. It differs in the structured preparation and exploration that precede generation, in the persistent documents that make design reasoning explicit and reusable, and in the verification integrated at defined checkpoints. Vibe Coding survives inside the method as an exploratory mode, useful for probing the possibility space before committing.

_Spec-Driven Development_ names the software industry's own corrective to the same reliability problem. Since 2025, agentic coding frameworks such as GitHub's Spec Kit[^5] have converged on maintaining specifications as the source of truth from which agents implement; a first process taxonomy of these frameworks exists (Macedo 2026). The convergence corroborates the premise that documents govern agents better than prompts do, and it sharpens what Promptotyping is. The definition here predates the label and grew from scholarly editing practice (Section 2.6), and the object differs. Spec-Driven Development specifies software products; Promptotyping specifies research artefacts derived from modelled research data, and it binds the specification to the scholarly verification duties of Section 6.2, which product specifications do not carry.

_Context Rot_ is the empirically documented degradation of LLM performance with growing input length (Hong et al. 2025). It supplies the technical rationale for the Distillation phase, whose principle is maximum information with minimum tokens, and for the two-dimensional account of data formats in Section 2.3.

_The Critical Expert in the Loop_ (Pollin 2025c) names the role this method presupposes, distinguished from the generic human in the loop by requiring both domain expertise and awareness of LLM-specific failure modes. Two failure modes are structural in current models. Sycophancy is the tendency to agree with user assumptions instead of challenging them (Sharma et al. 2023; Fanous et al. 2025). Confabulation is the generation of plausible but false outputs. The Critical Expert's task extends beyond checking outputs to the possibility space that was not explored, the questions not asked and the alternatives not generated. This role is the human side of the division of labour the method installs (Section 3.4). Context engineering and the Context Rot rationale become operative in the Distillation phase of Section 3.2, and Vibe Coding survives there as the exploratory mode of Exploration.

### 2.6 Genealogy

The method's origin lies in my doctoral research on modelling and operationalising historical information (Pollin 2025b). The dissertation modelled historical financial records, account books and their semantics, for digital scholarly editions, using Semantic Web ontologies and TEI-XML. Its methodological core, there discussed as Scholar-Centred Design, combined user-centred design with requirements engineering. In deep-dive sessions conducted with project partners between 2017 and 2020, historians of different source types worked through their materials, and the sessions produced personas, epics, and user stories in the fixed requirements-engineering format; these fed the data models and visualisations built on them, culminating in the Bookkeeping Ontology. The articulation ran both ways. The sessions required the historians to make their domain understanding explicit, an early instance of the boundary-object effect of Section 2.4. The dissertation's argument ran from captured user stories to better research interfaces. What it could not change was the cost of the final step; between the operationalised data and any interface through which a historian could have browsed and filtered it stood the wall of development capacity. The experience of that gap, holding well-modelled data that no one could see, is where Promptotyping began.

The dissertation's information-theoretic premise had placed a cognitive agent at the centre of the passage from data to knowledge, an agent whose knowledge structures give data context and significance (Pollin 2025b). Promptotyping externalises such knowledge structures into documents, and in the method the machine joins the human as a second agent working over them. Frontier LLMs changed the cost structure of exactly that final step. The mapping from research data and structured context onto a research artefact, previously manual implementation work, became executable by the model itself. Promptotyping names the working mode under this changed condition, in which the structured context carries the design knowledge and the artefact is generated from it. A requirements document that represents specifications as user stories is the continuation of Scholar-Centred Design by other means; a data document is the continuation of data modelling. In this sense the method generalises a practice that scholarly editing already had, and its lineage runs through the humanities. Software engineering later converged on specification-driven agent workflows from its own side (Section 2.5); the two lines meet in the working mode without sharing an origin.

A dated waypoint completes the genealogy. The workshop demonstration of June 2023 (Section 1) shows the capability existing before the method. LLM-supported visualisation of research data was possible, for a hybrid practitioner driving each step by hand.

I am the hybrid scholar-developer on whom the field's existing answers to the translation problem rely. This is a bias to declare and simultaneously the point of the argument. Promptotyping externalises what hybrid practitioners do into documents and agentic tooling, so that non-hybrid scholars gain a working substitute for a bounded part of the competence they lack, and hybrid ones multiply the reach of the competence they have. Whether the externalisation works for non-hybrid scholars is an empirical question, and the teaching cases of Section 5.4 are where this paper's evidence for it lies.

## 3. The Method

### 3.1 Status and Provenance

Promptotyping was distilled from practice, and the definition below consolidates that practice after the fact. The documented projects of Section 5 were built across a period in which the method's vocabulary stabilised, and the earliest of them were conducted intuitively, with the formal document set emerging partly in retrospect; in the earliest projects the first Promptotyping Documents were written after the fact, and only later projects placed structured documents before implementation (Section 5). The method defined below is therefore the consolidated form, the description of a practice at the point where it became teachable. I consider this the normal epistemology of method formation, comparable to how qualitative research methods are codified from exemplary studies, and state it so that the evidence can be read correctly; the cases validate the consolidated method to different degrees, and the later cases validate it more strictly than the early ones.

### 3.2 The Four Phases

Promptotyping is iterative context engineering. Its unit of work is a small set of versioned Markdown documents that together constitute the specification of the artefact, and its central loop iterates over these documents; the implementation is regenerated from them downstream. The process moves through four recurring phases.

**Preparation** collects and structures all materials before technical decisions are made. The materials are research data in original formats, documentation of standards and data models, research questions, and domain knowledge. The phase typically begins with requirements engineering, articulating user stories and data-to-question mappings, whether from the researcher's own domain knowledge or from collaborative Scholar-Centred Design sessions. Vagueness here cascades through all subsequent phases. The phase ends when the repository structure exists, source data is accessible, and initial requirements are documented as a structured starting point.

**Exploration** probes the interface between data and research context. Can the abstract research question be concretely mapped to the available data structure? The LLM generates mapping hypotheses, which data fields correspond to which analytical categories, which visualisation types fit the data, and where the data is insufficient for the intended purpose. This phase has an epistemic dimension beyond feasibility. The model generates options the researcher would not have designed independently; the researcher's role is to evaluate them against domain criteria, a different cognitive mode from classical development, where one must conceive a solution before implementing it. Understanding what the data cannot support is as valuable as knowing what is possible; dead ends are positive findings. The phase ends when there is a documented understanding of what is feasible, what is not, and why.

**Distillation** compresses what Exploration taught into the document set. This is the phase in which context engineering happens in the strict sense, the deliberate construction of the context a model will work from, under the principle of maximum information with minimum tokens (Section 2.5, Context Rot). The compression is not neutral; encoding decisions are epistemic decisions that determine what information will be available to all downstream steps. The phase ends when a new agent instance, given only the documents and the data, could reconstruct the project's logic without additional explanation.

**Implementation** hands the documents to an agentic coding tool operating inside the project repository and verifies the derived artefact. The researcher steers the generation of code through structured context and evaluative feedback, along three mechanisms. Deterministic feedback comes from schema validation, test suites, and builds. Visual feedback comes from screenshots of the running artefact, which the model interprets through multimodal capabilities. Expert feedback comes from the Critical Expert's judgement of whether the output is factually correct, domain-appropriate, and aligned with the research question, the only mechanism that can detect confabulation. A fourth form extends the expert feedback and is beginning to be delegated, an agent instance testing the running artefact against the user stories and acceptance criteria of the requirements document by operating it. The loop from Implementation back to Distillation is the method's signature. When the artefact is wrong, the specification was wrong or incomplete, and it is the specification that is fixed; corrections go into the documents, and the artefact is regenerated. New knowledge produced during implementation, unexpected data characteristics, performance constraints, design refinements, is written back into the documents, which stay living artefacts.

The phases recur; a single pass through them is the exception. Iteration ends when the artefact satisfies the acceptance criteria of the requirements document and the verification checks of Section 6.2. The exit point is a spectrum, from a rapid functional prototype produced in hours to a multi-stage pipeline with verification interfaces at every stage; what determines the appropriate stopping point is the research question.

The method operates in two modes, distinguished by how the work is organised, independently of project size. The default is a single researcher with a single agent instance. In complex projects, the work itself acquires the structure of a research organisation, with multiple parallel tasks, differentiated roles, and formalised handovers; there, a lead agent coordinates sub-agents with defined permissions and knowledge zones, and the researcher's methodological burden shifts from steering one agent to designing a small research team. The cognitive demands of that mode are real and are named in Section 6.4; the four phases apply to both modes, and this paper's evidence lies overwhelmingly in the first.

### 3.3 The Promptotyping Documents

The documents are the method's primary artefact. A recurring core set has stabilised across the documented projects, maintained in a `knowledge/` folder within the repository:

- **`data.md`** describes the structure, semantics, formats, and idiosyncrasies of the research data, including its gaps and uncertainties. Typically produced by compressing exploratory data analysis into a compact description; from samples of the actual JSON, XML, or CSV, iterations extract and condense the structure.
- **`requirements.md`** (in some projects `specification.md` or `user-stories.md`) holds functional and non-functional requirements, represented as user stories of scholarly work with acceptance criteria, the direct continuation of Scholar-Centred Design (Section 2.6).
- **`design.md`** records interface and visualisation decisions with their reasons. This is where Drucker's critique becomes operational; conventions are chosen and justified, or rejected, and the model's defaults hold no authority.
- **`journal.md`** is the session-by-session record of iterations, what was tried, what failed, which model produced what, which decisions were taken and why. The journal is what makes the process citable and criticisable after the fact.
- The **action layer**, a `CLAUDE.md` or equivalent configuration in the repository root, carries the implementation directives handed to the agent, including the technical guidelines of Section 4. In mature projects this file functions as agent socialisation, embedding the agent with a value system that guides decisions in unforeseen situations.

Not every project needs every document at full depth; a small tool repository may consolidate several functions into one file, and a multi-stage pipeline may split them further. What is constant is the double addressee. Every document must be readable by the scholar, because reading is how verification happens, and actionable by the agent, because the documents are the context from which the artefact is derived. This double function disciplines the writing; a data description too vague for a model to work against is usually also too vague to count as scholarly documentation.

The double function is grounded in what kind of object these documents are. In the vocabulary of the modelling tradition, a Promptotyping Document is a conceptual model of the project, a representation of domain ideas that rests on natural language, transports its semantics through the shared vocabulary of a community of practice, and is semi-formal in the precise sense that it balances practical communication against residual ambiguity (Mayr and Thalheim 2021; Pollin 2025b). The tradition behind this vocabulary supplies three properties of models that the general model theory of Stachowiak (1973) established and my dissertation already used to frame modelling. A model represents an original; it abbreviates, carrying only the attributes relevant to its purpose; and it is pragmatic, made for someone, for a time span, and for a use. All three carry weight here. Representation is what makes a document set mappable onto further artefacts at all, and the generated artefact is in this strict sense a model of the model, the second link in a chain of representations that begins in the research context. Abbreviation gives Distillation its theoretical licence, because omitting what is irrelevant to the purpose is constitutive of modelling and precedes any context-window limit. The pragmatic property names the double addressee; a document is written for the scholar and for the agent, for this phase of the process, and it is revised when addressee or purpose changes. Conceptual models specify requirements and guide system creation; a document that describes the project explicitly enough is, by that fact, its specification. The formula has a lineage. Knowledge engineering defined an ontology as an explicit specification of a conceptualisation (Gruber 1993), and a Promptotyping Document set is exactly that in natural language, a specification standing at the semi-formal end of the representational spectrum whose formal end, ontologies in OWL and RDF, my earlier work occupied (Section 2.6). What has changed is the cost of the step that follows. The path from a semi-formal conceptual model to a running system used to pass through formalisation, into data models, schemas, and code, and that formalisation was the human translator's work (Section 2.4). Frontier models operationalise semi-formal conceptual models directly, and because they execute that second mapping stochastically, its fidelity is not guaranteed by construction; the ambiguity that semi-formality leaves open, and the variance that stochastic execution adds, are what the Critical Expert's verification addresses, and Section 6.2 builds on this ground.

The document set rests, finally, on a substrate condition. Markdown-based knowledge structures, from a repository's `knowledge/` folder to a full research vault, represent a project conceptually in the one format frontier agents process natively, plain text readable by humans and machines without conversion, versionable in git, and navigable file by file. This places the substrate at a midpoint of the spectrum of Section 2.3. What Section 2.3 identified as the explication work that survives the change of carrier is, concretely, the structuring effort these documents carry, and a frontier model reads that semi-formal structure directly. Direct processability does not suspend the token economy of Section 2.3; what enters the context remains the curated, distilled subset. What it removes is the format boundary between the scholar's description and the machine's input.

The research data community has a document layer of its own, and the comparison locates the Promptotyping Documents precisely. Data management plans describe how a project's data will be handled, and the community's own diagnosis is that they tend to end as compliance documents, written for funders and then left unread; the call for machine-actionable DMPs (Miksa et al. 2019) demands documents that infrastructure can act on. Promptotyping Documents are machine-actionable in a stronger, operative sense. An agent implements from them in every session, and a document no agent can work from gets revised for exactly that reason.

Across the documented projects, three analytically distinct types of Promptotyping Documents have emerged, and the distinction has a foundation older than the method. Knowledge engineering, the discipline that since the expert systems of the 1980s has worked on making expert knowledge machine-processable, classically distinguishes declarative knowledge, what is the case, from procedural knowledge, how something is to be done; process records form a third, chronological kind. The typology maps onto this distinction directly. **Knowledge Documents** are declarative, among them `data.md`, `requirements.md`, `design.md`, editorial guidelines, and mapping rules. They describe what is known about the data, the domain, and the research context, are curated by the Critical Expert, and constitute the persistent artefact of the context engineering process; the knowledge they encode survives changes in code, model versions, and tools. **Process Documents** are chronological, `journal.md` and its derivatives, such as curated learnings files. Together with the git history they form a context memory that enables reliable session resumption; the journal documents why, the commits document what. **Action Documents** are imperative, the action layer, and in multi-agent projects the role definitions and orchestration rules. They describe what agents may do within the project.

The typology is analytical, without rigid boundaries, and it earns its place through a diagnostic function. When the model produces factually incorrect output, the Knowledge Documents need revision. When the output is formally wrong, in structure, style, or format, the Action Documents need adjustment. When the decision logic is unclear or inconsistent, the Process Documents need to be consulted or updated. This triage directs attention to the right document type and accelerates debugging. An inventory across the documented repositories classified their documents into the three types; the distribution shows the primacy of knowledge formalisation in the method, and the observation that output quality tracks the precision of the domain documentation recurs across the record (Section 5.5).

A second distinction cuts across the types. Documents are either hand-curated or deterministically generated. Hand-curated documents are written and maintained by the Critical Expert, possibly with LLM assistance, and represent judgements that cannot be derived from data alone. Deterministically generated documents are rendered from source data by scripts and committed alongside the curated layer; they are Knowledge Documents in function but outputs of a pipeline in production. The motivation is the token economy of Section 2.3: full source corpora overflow the context window, while a deterministic aggregation, run once and refreshed when the source changes, produces compact descriptions that fit the context budget and trace cleanly back to the data. In one edition project, a set of Python scripts traverses the TEI-XML corpus and the project's schema and renders Markdown documents describing corpus structure, anomalies, and schema-versus-corpus mismatches; the same pattern recurs wherever source data is large or heterogeneous. Generated documents carry frontmatter naming them as generated and are not edited by hand. This makes Distillation a build-time stage as well as a writing act, governed by the same reproducibility logic as the code it supports.

The document practice has begun to harden into a metadata practice. The recurring document functions have been consolidated into a versioned, publicly published catalogue of templates on the method's documentation site (https://dhcraft.org/Promptotyping/), itself an application of the method to its own artefacts; each template was extracted from the recurring form a function takes across at least two repositories, and its requirements were checked against the empirical record instead of prescribed in advance. The clearest instance of this self-correction concerns the mandatory frontmatter core. An earlier specification required eight fields; a survey of the real knowledge bases found that not a single repository satisfied that core in full. The core was reduced to the six fields the practice actually carries, `title`, `project`, `method`, `status`, `created`, `updated`, on the principle that a requirement the practice silently ignores documents nothing. Each template carries a permanent public address, and a repository declares its method through a `template:` field in its document frontmatter that resolves to the authoritative specification; a coding agent that encounters the field can retrieve the full template without prior knowledge of the project. The repository explains itself to the agent that reads it. Finally, the catalogue has promoted an observed habit into a document function of its own. Several projects had begun to check their own empirical and novelty claims against the raw data before using them externally. These verification documents record what claim is checked, against what evidence, by what procedure, and with what verdict; the quantitative claims of Section 5 are themselves covered by one.

The nearest relative of this self-description practice in FAIR infrastructure is RO-Crate, which packages research artefacts with machine-readable metadata for repositories and registries (Soiland-Reyes et al. 2022). The difference lies in the direction of description. An RO-Crate describes a finished research package retrospectively for the infrastructure that stores it; the frontmatter and `template:` layer describes a repository prospectively and operatively, for the agent about to work in it. The same frontmatter fields can in turn be rendered into the citation metadata that harvesters read, a point Section 4.1 develops.

### 3.4 A Worked Example: The Stefan Zweig HTR Pipeline

The SZD-HTR pipeline[^7] produces machine-readable text from the digitised estate of Stefan Zweig at the Literaturarchiv Salzburg, tens of thousands of facsimile scans of manuscripts, typescripts, and correspondence, described by TEI metadata from the Stefan Zweig Digital platform. Preparation fixed the scholarly purpose and the corpus. The estate should become readable and searchable in diplomatic transcription, without normalisation, and the TEI metadata supplies each object's language, type, and title as machine-readable context. Exploration ran vision-model transcription experiments and produced the analytically decisive finding as a dead end. The model's self-assessment is worthless; it reported high confidence for clean typescripts and for multi-page Kurrent manuscripts alike, and across tens of thousands of transcribed characters it almost never produced the uncertainty markers its prompts explicitly demanded. Distillation turned this finding into documents. A verification concept specifies a ground-truth strategy, automatic quality signals that flag objects for review from the outside since the model will not flag itself, cross-model consensus with a judging model, and agent-based vision checks; an annotation protocol defines the transcription conventions precisely enough that two annotators working independently produce comparable error rates, with the reason for each convention recorded; and the TEI context drives the automatic assignment of prompt variants per object type. The design holds uncertainty visible, uncertain readings, illegible passages, deletions, and insertions are mandated markup, and the viewer renders every transcription beside its facsimile, the Drucker mechanism of Section 2.1 applied to transcription. Implementation derived the pipeline and the viewer through the agent, and verification runs as a three-status model, human-checked (every page read against the facsimile) ranks above agent-checked (a vision model compared image and transcript) ranks above unchecked, the authority ordering of Section 6.2 as project practice; when a human corrects, the model's original is preserved in an edit history. The loop back into the documents is on record, the review model itself was consolidated from four tiers to three by an operator decision documented in the knowledge base, and the journal carries the iterations and models used.

The example shows the division of labour the method installs. The scholar models, specifies, and verifies; the agent implements. This division is defended on normative grounds. I do not claim that modelling, specifying, and verifying are things generative systems cannot do; I claim that they are the work that must remain with scholars because it carries scholarly responsibility, in the same sense in which authorship carries responsibility regardless of what tools contributed to a text.

## 4. The Artefact Type

### 4.1 Self-Contained Static Web Tools

The artefacts Promptotyping produces are, by deliberate default, self-contained static web tools, a set of HTML, CSS, and JavaScript files with the research data embedded or loaded from flat files, deployable on any static host and runnable from a local folder. Three properties motivate the default. Static artefacts are generable in one pass by current agentic tools, which keeps the derivation from documents to artefact inspectable. They are publishable without infrastructure, which matches the resource situation of the researchers the method addresses. And they are durable in exactly the sense the field's own sustainability discourse demands; minimal computing (Risam and Gil 2022) and the Endings Project (Holmes and Takeda 2023) have argued that the digital artefacts most likely to survive are those with the fewest moving parts, no server-side dependencies, and no build chains that rot.

The technical guidelines follow from these properties. Vanilla JavaScript is the default; frameworks age fast, resist inspection, and add nothing that exploratory instruments need. Where a problem is algorithmically deep, a single library may be vendored locally under a compromise rule with four criteria. The problem is genuinely beyond reasonable vanilla implementation; the library is self-contained and vendorable without a build step; its licence permits redistribution; and its removal path is documented. Heavy computation is precomputed; the artefact ships derived data, and the pipelines stay upstream. No external calls at runtime; the artefact must work offline, which is at once a longevity, a privacy, and a security property. And every artefact carries a provenance declaration stating that it was generated, from which documents, with which models and tools, and how it was verified; the artefact discloses its conditions of production the way an edition discloses its editorial principles.

The artefact type can also be measured against the field's standard for research software, the FAIR4RS principles (Chue Hong et al. 2022), and the result is uneven in an instructive way. Accessibility holds by construction; a static artefact and its source are retrievable over HTTPS without proprietary tooling. Reusability is where the method overdelivers. The detailed provenance the principles ask for, why and how the software came to be and who contributed what (R1.2), is exactly what journal, documents, and repository history record as a by-product of working, and few conventional research software projects produce it as completely. Findability, by contrast, fails by default. An audit of one documented project, the worked example of Section 3.4, against the full criteria found no persistent identifier, no versioned releases, and no machine-readable citation metadata, the standard condition of prototypes that were never formally published; the audit is deposited as a Verification Document in that repository (`knowledge/verification-fair4rs.md`). Closing the gap is publication work, separable from the method. An archived release with a DOI, and citation metadata rendered deterministically from the document frontmatter the repositories already carry, close it without touching the artefact.

### 4.2 A Typology of Promptotyping Interfaces

The browser-based interfaces built across the documented projects sort by the epistemic function they serve; data format and visualisation technique proved secondary as ordering principles. The classification addresses a gap across three traditions. Visual analytics process models come closest to an epistemic framework and were not designed as interface classifications (Sacha et al. 2014); digital humanities taxonomies such as TaDiRAH classify research activities, while the epistemic work an interface enables lies outside their scope (Borek et al. 2016; Unsworth 2000); and Collins and Ferguson's (1993) epistemic forms, target knowledge structures paired with strategies for constructing them, organise inquiry by the kind of knowledge produced, the principle the typology operationalises for research data interfaces. The mismatch with TaDiRAH is instructive in its detail. Capture, exploration, and editing have counterparts among its research activities; verification and audit do not appear, and the field's workflow registry, the SSH Open Marketplace, likewise records workflows as chains of TaDiRAH-annotated activities without a vocabulary for the artefacts that carry them (Barbot et al. 2024). TaDiRAH's standing as the field's most widely adopted classification, and its maintenance problems, are documented in the tool-registry literature (Grallert et al. 2026). Five categories have emerged.

_Verification Interfaces_ check pipeline outputs at defined milestones, making intermediate results comparable and correctable before errors propagate downstream. A multi-source OCR comparison viewer with layout overlay is the paradigmatic case. The function corresponds to making the constructed nature of data visible (Drucker 2011), applied here to the production stages before the final representation.

_Exploration Interfaces_ enable pattern discovery in existing structured research data through coordinated multiple views (Roberts 2007). Here the interface is the primary research artefact, the means through which the data becomes analytically accessible; this category operationalises Whitelaw's generous interfaces.

_Edition Interfaces_ render scholarly digital editions with facsimile synchronisation, TEI text display, and editorial correction capabilities. Interface decisions in digital editions are editorial decisions (Pierazzo 2015), and the edition is itself theorised as an interface, GUI for readers and API for machines (Bleier et al. 2018).

_Capture Interfaces_ enable structured data input, annotation, or metadata creation within a research workflow. They produce new data, and in doing so they shape the data model itself; an annotation interface reveals what the current model can and cannot express, and so becomes an instrument of iterative model refinement.

_Audit Interfaces_ make an entire research process inspectable and traceable, rendering the path that produced the results alongside the results, with assessment comparisons, divergence cases, and statistics that let readers evaluate the process itself.

The categories are not mutually exclusive; a single pipeline project may combine Verification Interfaces at its stages, an Edition Interface as its reader, and Capture elements in its curation editor. What the typology makes visible is that the epistemic function determines the interface design. A TEI-XML dataset may require an Edition Interface, an Exploration Interface, or both, depending on the research question that requirements engineering has surfaced; this is the empirical reason why the monolithic one-size-fits-all dashboard fails; a question-specific, generated interface preserves the model-specific detail the dashboard flattens (Section 1).

### 4.3 Limits of the Format, and the Handover Point

The limits of the format are part of its definition. Client-side processing bounds the data volume, and the bound moves with the stack. With flat JSON or CSV files, experience across the documented cases places the comfortable range up to the order of ten thousand records [to verify]; columnar formats with WebAssembly query engines shift that range by orders of magnitude while the artefact stays static and self-contained, at the price of a vendored engine that the compromise rule above must cover. Beyond the bound, precomputation and sampling carry more of the load. Static artefacts do not support collaborative or server-mediated workflows, and persistence beyond the browser is out of scope. These limits are the contour of the artefact type. Where a project outgrows them, it has outgrown prototyping and entered software development, and at this boundary the responsibility changes hands; sustained software with maintenance obligations, multi-user workflows, and institutional deployment is the domain of Research Software Engineering, with its standards for testing, sustainability, and maintenance. The method's document set does not lose its value at this boundary; it becomes the handover package (Section 6.5).

### 5.3 One Case per Epistemic Function

**Verification: the ZBZ OCR/TEI pipeline.** Source data: 286 PDF scans, approximately 4,150 pages of estate materials of the Zentralbibliothek Zürich. Documents: 13 knowledge documents governing a nine-stage pipeline; over 30 Python scripts generated and maintained entirely through the agent. Epistemic yield: the interfaces interrupt the error cascade that characterises multi-stage automated pipelines; expert judgement is applied at defined milestones, where it discriminates. OCR processing cost less than 10 EUR; the initial build took approximately six weeks (128 commits) and development has continued since. Limits: the pipeline is the project where the method stops being lightweight; 13 documents and nine stages are infrastructure, and Section 6.4 counts the cost.

**Exploration: the CorrespExplorer.**[^8] Source data: 11,576 CMIF-encoded letters. Documents: the first complete vault of the record, initially 7 documents, 34 user stories, 46 journal phases, 74+ tests. Artefact: 12 coordinated visualisation views over the correspondence metadata. Epistemic yield: the Exploration phase evaluated six visualisation types and discarded two with documented reasoning, an instance of the model generating a possibility space and the expert pruning it against domain criteria; the project also produced the record's first empirical observation of context degradation at roughly half the advertised context window. Limits: the views inherit the conventions of correspondence visualisation, and only the design document stands between the artefact and Drucker's critique.

**Edition: the Notker Psalm commentary.** Source data: a DOCX sample page of Psalm 2, thirteen verses of the Old High German Psalm commentary by Notker III of St. Gallen (ca. 950–1022), in which Latin psalm text, Old High German translation, and exegetical commentary interleave in one textual flow. Documents: a nine-document knowledge base whose editorial-guidelines document fixes the TEI encoding of every text phenomenon, the three functional layers, the constant code-switching between Latin and Old High German, the patristic source apparatus, and the marginal sigles. TEI-XML is the canonical source, and the pipeline derives from it the JSON the interface reads. Artefact: an edition interface that renders the three functional layers as independently toggleable, sets Notker's text line-faithfully beside the commissioner's modern German translation, and carries in a configurable three-slot layout the patristic source apparatus, the IIIF facsimile of manuscript CSg 0021 through e-codices, a synoptic comparison of five psalm-text witnesses, and the Wiener Notker as a parallel tradition. Epistemic yield: the interface separates the functional layers that the critical print editions of Tax and Sehrt leave entangled, and it operationalises an editorial disambiguation the print apparatus cannot state. The sigle R resolves to the Romanum psalter beside a psalm citation and to Remigius of Auxerre beside a commentary segment, and the data model encodes this resolution by section type. The editorial-guidelines document carries the philological competence behind such decisions. Limits: this externalisation is also the verification burden. The R-disambiguation is recorded as still awaiting the commissioning scholar's confirmation, and where the guidelines encode a wrong rule, no implementer's intuition catches it. Verification falls to the domain expert, and the case sharpens Section 6.2.

**Capture: M³GIM.**[^9] Source data: archival data in RiC-O/JSON-LD. Documents: 25+ sessions, 49 documented decisions at the verified build state, the register has since grown. Artefact: an eight-tab archive explorer with structured input. Epistemic yield: the interface reveals what the current data model can and cannot express, and the data model evolves through it; the interface is an instrument of model refinement before it is a presentation. Limits: the boundary between model refinement and model drift is held by the decision register, which must be maintained.

**Audit: FemPrompt SozArb.**[^10] Source data: 326 academic papers in social work research, beyond the digital humanities. Documents and infrastructure: a 505-file vault; a five-stage LLM pipeline with dual-track assessment, human experts and LLM independently on identical ten-category schemas. Artefact: an evidence companion visualising the human-LLM comparison with confusion matrices, agreement statistics, and divergence cases. Epistemic yield: the audit interface makes the research process itself inspectable; the quantified divergence between human and machine assessment (68% versus 42% inclusion) is the primary research output. Limits: the audit depends on the schemas being genuinely identical across tracks, a design property the documents must guarantee.

The typology above rests on my own practice. ### 5.5 Reading the Evidence

Two observations cut across the record. First, the documented cases converge technically on vanilla JavaScript, static deployment, and frontend-only architecture throughout. This uniformity is partly the guidelines and plausibly partly model bias towards dominant conventions; Section 6.4 treats it as an open problem. Second, the quality of the artefacts tracks the domain expertise invested in the documents more than any other variable visible in the record. The projects with the deepest domain modelling produced the instruments with the most epistemic yield, and the failures cluster where documents were vague. The cases are evidence for the method's central premise, that the scholarly work of modelling and specification is where the value enters.

## 6. Discussion

### 6.1 Disposable Process, Durable Artefact

The sandcastle and the archive appear to pull in opposite directions; Section 2.1 embraced impermanence as an epistemic virtue, Section 4 built for longevity. The tension dissolves once process and publication are distinguished. Within the research process, promptotypes are disposable in exactly Hinrichs' sense. Iterations are cheap to discard, regeneration from amended documents is the normal case, and the value of a failed iteration is what it taught the document set. The artefact a project publishes, by contrast, enters the scholarly record and inherits its obligations; hence the Endings-compliant format, the provenance declaration, and the versioned documents from which it can be understood and criticised. Disposability governs the making, durability the made, and the documents connect the two, because they persist across discarded iterations and accompany the published artefact as its specification.

### 6.2 Verification

The obvious objection to generated research instruments is quality, and the answer developed across this paper is verification anchored in the documents. It operates at three levels. Data fidelity is checked by sampling the artefact's renderings against the source data, supported where possible by deterministic checks, schema validation, and test suites. Requirement satisfaction is checked against the acceptance criteria of the requirements document, which is what user stories with acceptance criteria are for. Design conformance is checked by reading the artefact against the design document, which is where tool criticism becomes a routine act; the reviewer of a promptotype has, for once, the tool's explicit assumptions in hand.

In practice these levels sort the work into zones of agent autonomy. Where correctness is deterministically verifiable, validation, tests, builds, the agent works unsupervised, because the feedback loop is closed. Where outputs require expert inspection, OCR results, annotations, transformations, verification interfaces exist precisely to make intermediate results visible before errors propagate. Between the deterministic zone and the expert zone, an adversarial machine review can be interposed, a model instructed to attack an output or a claim against its sources; the evidence companion of Section 5.1 practises this as a checking instance of its own, released case by case and ranked below human verification in authority. And interpretation, contextualisation, and the judgement of whether the right questions are being asked remain human; determining whether a named person in one medieval document is the same as a similarly named person in another requires historical contextualisation the model cannot perform, a boundary the documented edition project marks explicitly. What the method cannot do is make verification automatic, and it should not pretend to. Verification is precisely the scholarly labour that the division of responsibility assigns to the human, and its cost is the honest price of the possibility claim.

### 6.3 Reproducibility and Model Dependence

A Promptotyping process generates hundreds of thousands of tokens of interaction. Archiving this interaction is neither practical nor meaningful, because models are non-deterministic; identical prompts do not produce identical outputs, and regenerating an artefact from identical documents with a different model, or the same model later, yields a different implementation. Reproducibility therefore attaches to the documents, the process record, and the deterministic parts of the pipeline; the binary identity of artefacts lies beyond it. What can be documented is the decision trail, what was tried, what was decided, why, and what was discarded, encoded in the Promptotyping Documents and the version history, analogous to process documentation in qualitative research (Abdurahman et al. 2025 provide related recommendations for LLM use in social science). The deterministic layer strengthens this. Generated documents and transformation scripts re-run identically, so the path from source data to shipped derived data is repeatable even where the generation of the code was not. The provenance declaration in each artefact exists to make all of this explicit. This is not a claim about the traceability of LLM reasoning, which remains opaque; the claim is narrower, that the human decisions that shaped the project are reconstructable.

Read through FAIR4RS, this relocates reusability. For conventional research software the reusable object is the code. For a promptotype the reusable object is the document set together with the data, because a competent agent regenerates the artefact from them while the code is a disposable derivation; what the principles address as software reuse is here carried by the specification layer. What remains open is findability, and it connects the method to current registry work. Community-curated tool registries built on Wikidata (Grallert et al. 2026) offer an infrastructure through which small generated instruments could become findable without their creators maintaining a catalogue, and the deterministic path from document frontmatter to citation metadata (Section 4.1) is what such registries would harvest.

### 6.4 Limits

The limits are of five kinds. The evidential limit comes first. The validation offered here is a set of documented cases with inspectable process records from a single practitioner, without a control condition, and the observed gains confound method effects with model capability, which improved substantially across the documented period; the method evolves with the models, which is simultaneously a strength and a source of instability. Claims about what the method achieves for others rest most heavily on the teaching cases, and documented third-party use is still thin. Second, the format limits of Section 4.3 bound the artefact type. Third, the convention problem remains only partially solved. Design documents can override model defaults case by case, but the pull of learned conventions is constant, and a method that requires vigilance has merely made the problem addressable. Fourth, access is unequal. Effective Promptotyping currently requires frontier-model subscriptions, and this creates inequalities the method does not resolve; the dynamic is an instance of asymmetric amplification, in which those with deeper expertise, better-structured data, and paid access benefit disproportionately. The method makes this visible. Fifth, the profession clause bears repeating as a limit. The method does not produce Research Software Engineers, and a practitioner without domain expertise gains little, because the documents such a practitioner can write are exactly as good as the knowledge behind them. Sustained multi-agent operation adds a practical concern. The documented record explicitly characterises monitoring several parallel agents as unsustainable over extended periods.

### 6.5 Transferability

The paper has argued from the humanities, and the transfer claim can now be stated precisely. Nothing in the method's mechanics is humanities-specific. Documents as boundary objects, the specifier-verifier role, the static artefact type, and the three verification levels apply wherever structured research data meets a researcher without development capacity, and two documented cases already operate outside the digital humanities, in economic research data and in social work research. What is humanities-specific is the difficulty profile the method was forged against, heterogeneous formats, interpretative modelling, semantic density, and uncertainty as a property of the data. Fields whose data is schema-regular and whose conventions of representation are settled face an easier version of the problem, so the transfer is an inference in the favourable direction, offered as such; testing it is work for practitioners of those fields, and the published templates are built to be taken.

One transfer path deserves naming because it requires no generative tooling at all. The document set is also a handover format towards humans. A project that reaches the prototype boundary of Section 4.3 hands a Research Software Engineer a versioned specification with data description, user stories, acceptance criteria, design rationale, and process record; the boundary-object property of the documents holds towards human developers exactly as it holds towards agents. The method thus complements the RSE profession at both ends. Below the boundary it serves researchers the profession's capacity does not reach, and at the boundary it improves the handover to it.

## 7. Conclusion

Promptotyping is a methodological answer to a gap the digital humanities have documented for two decades, between scholars who model research data and the interactive instruments through which that data could be explored. The method's claims are two. Versioned documents can carry the translation of research data into research artefacts, concentrating scholarly work where scholarly responsibility lies, in modelling, specifying, and verifying; and the same documents function as boundary objects that stabilise interdisciplinary translation in an inspectable form, towards coding agents and towards human developers alike. The artefacts are prototypes in the strict sense, disposable in the making, durable in publication, bounded by the handover point at which Research Software Engineering begins. The epistemic centre of the practice is the modelled data; the twenty-year investment of the field in standards, ontologies, and semantic modelling is what the method renders productive, through a different mechanism than the Semantic Web envisioned but towards the end it named. This paper is the method's canonical definition; the evidence layer is published as a versioned companion with claim-level anchors, and the templates are public. The method was distilled from practice, and testing it further, in the humanities and beyond, remains open.
