# Promptotyping. Making Research Data Explorable through Iterative Context and Agentic Engineering

Christopher Pollin (Digital Humanities Craft OG, Graz)

## Abstract

Whether structured research data becomes interactively explorable has so far depended on people rather than tools, and this dependence is a strength of research practice before it is a constraint. The value of research data rests on human work; it is carefully modelled and operationalised, made by people for people, and no generative system replaces this foundation. What has limited exploration is not this human basis but scarce development capacity and the costly translation between domain scholars and programmers, to which fields have answered with professional intermediaries, hybrid scholar-developers, and team-based collaboration. This paper introduces Promptotyping, an iterative, document-driven context engineering method situated within agentic engineering, and argues from the perspective of the humanities, whose heterogeneous and interpretatively modelled data constitute the hard case for any such method. Promptotyping changes what is achievable in two ways. First, requirements, data descriptions, and design decisions are maintained as versioned documents from which LLM-based agentic tools derive the artefact; the method concentrates human expertise on modelling, specifying, and verifying, the work that must remain with scholars because it carries scholarly responsibility. Second, these documents function as boundary objects; they externalise domain knowledge in a form that scholars can read and verify and that agents can act upon, relocating interdisciplinary translation from conversation into a versioned, inspectable artefact. The results are not finished software but exploratory research instruments, typically self-contained static web tools. The contribution is methodological and conceptual, validated through a typology of documented cases rather than through quantitative evaluation. Exploration acquires scholarly value only when bound to verifiability.

## 1. Introduction

Research data does not explain itself. Between a dataset, however carefully curated, and the questions a scholar can put to it lies an interface, and building interfaces has been the business of people with development capacity. In the humanities this has meant that interactive, visual exploration of research data was largely reserved for infrastructure institutions and well-funded projects. An individual researcher with a corpus of annotated letters, a register of medieval transactions, or a collection of catalogued artefacts could model the data with great care and still lack any means of seeing it.

This dependence on people is a strength of research practice before it is a constraint. The value of research data rests on human work; data in the humanities is interpretatively modelled and operationalised, made by people for people, and no generative system replaces this foundation. On the contrary, the method presented here presupposes it, because well-modelled data is precisely what its context layer curates (Section 3.4). What has limited exploration is not the human basis of the data but development capacity, funding logic, project duration, and a translation problem between domain scholars and programmers that the field has documented for two decades. Edmond (2005) described the professional intermediary who mediates between scholarly requirements and technical implementation; Siemens (2009) analysed the team structures that digital humanities projects build to hold both competences together; Kemman (2021) reconstructed the trading zones in which historians and computational experts negotiate shared meaning. Each of these answers exists because the underlying problem is real. I infer, from the field's own accounts of intermediaries as scarce, of teams as bound to project funding, and of hybrid scholar-developers as a small population, that none of these answers reaches the individual researcher or the small project as a matter of course; this is an inference from the literature and from professional experience, not an established quantitative finding, and I mark it as such.

This paper introduces Promptotyping, an iterative, document-driven context engineering method situated within agentic engineering. Its central claim is a possibility claim, not an efficiency claim. Promptotyping makes a class of research acts achievable for individual researchers and small projects that was previously bound to project structures with dedicated development capacity; namely, the construction of interactive, visual instruments for exploring one's own research data. The paper deliberately advances no argument from speed or cost. I have no comparative measurements of development time or budget, I do not intend to produce any, and I hold that such measurements would miss the point; an engineering observation about efficiency is not a scholarly contribution. The contribution defended here is methodological and conceptual, and it stands or falls with two epistemic arguments. The first is that exploration itself is research, a position established in the information visualisation discourse of the digital humanities (Section 2.2). The second is that generated tools must remain verifiable, and that the method's documents are what makes them so (Sections 3, 4, and 6).

The argument proceeds from the humanities, and this perspective is not a limitation to be apologised for. Humanities research data is the hard case for any generative development method; it is heterogeneous, rarely standardised, interpretatively modelled, and dense with domain semantics that no model can be assumed to know. A method that holds up against archival registers, digital scholarly editions, and museum collections transfers more plausibly to tabular, schema-regular data than the reverse. The generalisation to research data as such is therefore itself an inference, argued in Section 6, and the evidence base of this paper remains the humanities.

Two delimitations frame everything that follows. First, the results of Promptotyping are not finished, maintained software but exploratory research artefacts; this modesty belongs to the argument (Section 4). Second, this paper is the canonical definition of the method. Promptotyping was developed inductively through practice; its precursors are two blog posts (Pollin [year-a]; Pollin [year-b]) and my dissertation (Pollin [year]), in which the idea originated. The method described in Section 3 is a consolidation of that practice, and Section 3.1 states openly what this consolidation entails.

The paper is organised as follows. Section 2 develops the epistemic frame, from research tools as epistemic artefacts through the information visualisation perspective to the method's origin in my own research and its function as an instrument of communication. Section 3 defines the method, its documents, its process, and its position within agentic engineering, including a worked example. Section 4 characterises the artefact type and its technical guidelines. Section 5 presents the documented use cases as a typology ordered by tool category, with the complete case inventory published as a versioned companion. Section 6 discusses the epistemic status of the artefacts, verification, transferability, and limits. Section 7 concludes.

## 2. The Epistemic Frame. Research Tools, Exploration, Communication

### 2.1 Tools as Epistemic Artefacts

The digital humanities have long argued that building is a way of knowing. Galey and Ruecker (2010) proposed that a prototype can embody an argument, that design decisions assert claims about the material they organise, and that a tool can therefore be read and criticised the way a monograph is. The INKE research programme extended this into a typology of prototypes as experiment, development, and provocation (Ruecker et al. 2015 [check exact reference]). If the prototype argues, then producing prototypes is not service work downstream of research but a research act in its own right.

The same discourse has developed the corrective to naive tool enthusiasm. Digital tool criticism (Koolen, van Gorp, and van Ossenbruggen 2019; van Es, Wieringa, and Schäfer 2018) demands that scholars reflect on what a tool does to the material, which assumptions it embeds, and whether it suits the research question at hand. Both strands matter here. The first grounds the claim that a generated exploratory tool can carry epistemic weight; the second supplies the standard that generated tools must meet, and Section 6 applies it to them explicitly.

### 2.2 The Information Visualisation Perspective

A second strand of the discourse concerns what interfaces to cultural material should do. Whitelaw (2015) contrasted the withholding search box with generous interfaces that show the collection, invite browsing, and reward the visitor who does not yet know what to ask. Hinrichs, Forlini, and Uhrig (2019) argued that visualisation in the humanities should be understood as a research process in its own right rather than as a presentation layer bolted onto finished results, and offered the sandcastle as its emblem, a construction whose value lies in the building and whose impermanence is not a defect. Windhager et al. (2019) surveyed the state of exploratory visualisation for cultural heritage collections and documented both the richness of the design space and how few projects can afford to enter it. Taken together, these positions establish that exploration is research, and they simultaneously expose the gap this paper addresses; the discourse knows what exploratory interfaces are good for, and the field can rarely build them.

Drucker's intervention is of a different kind and serves this paper as a touchstone rather than a citation of convenience. In Graphesis and in her earlier programmatic essay (Drucker 2011; Drucker 2014), she argued that the humanities deal in capta, in constructed and situated observations rather than given data, and that inherited visual conventions, the bar chart, the timeline, the map, smuggle positivist assumptions into humanistic material. This critique applies with particular force to LLM-generated visualisations, because language models reproduce exactly the standard conventions Drucker criticises; they have learned them from the aggregate of the web. A generative method that ignored this would automate the problem. Promptotyping's answer is structural rather than rhetorical. Because every artefact derives from explicit documents, visualisation decisions are lifted out of the model's defaults and into reasoned, versioned specifications that the scholar writes and can defend. A design document can state that a timeline must represent dating uncertainty as an interval rather than a point, that a network graph must not imply directionality the sources do not warrant, or that missing values must remain visible as missing. Whether a given project meets Drucker's standard is then an inspectable question about its documents, not a matter of trust in a model. Section 3.2 shows this mechanism in a concrete case, and Section 6 returns to its limits.

### 2.3 Origin in the Author's Own Research

The method has a biographical origin that is also a substantive one. My dissertation (Pollin [year]) modelled historical financial records, account books and their semantics, for digital scholarly editions, using Semantic Web ontologies and TEI-XML. The modelling succeeded; the exploration did not follow. Between the carefully operationalised data and any interface through which a historian could have browsed, filtered, and visualised it stood the familiar wall of development capacity. The experience of that gap, holding well-modelled data that no one could see, is where Promptotyping began.

The dissertation contributes more than the problem. Its methodological core, which I there discussed as scholar-centred design, consisted in modelling user stories of scholarly work and the workflows through which research data moves [placeholder; the derivation from scholar-centred design, user stories, and research data workflows to the Promptotyping document set is to be elaborated here once discussed]. The line from there to the method of Section 3 is direct; a REQUIREMENTS document that represents specifications as user stories is the continuation of modelled user stories by other means, and the DATA document is the continuation of data modelling. Promptotyping is in this sense not an import from software engineering into the humanities but a generalisation of a practice that scholarly editing already had.

A note of methodological reflexivity belongs here. I am myself the hybrid scholar-developer that the field's existing answers to the translation problem rely on. This is a bias to declare and simultaneously the point of the argument; Promptotyping generalises what hybrid practitioners do. It externalises the second competence into documents and agentic tooling, so that non-hybrid scholars gain a working substitute for the competence they lack, and hybrid ones multiply the reach of the competence they have.

### 2.4 Promptotyping as Communication

The translation problem between domain scholars and programmers is not an anecdote but a documented, persistent condition of the field. Edmond (2005) made the professional intermediary a figure of necessity; Siemens (2009) showed how much of digital humanities collaboration is the labour of keeping heterogeneous competences aligned; Kemman (2021) analysed digital history as a trading zone in which meaning must be continuously negotiated across disciplinary boundaries.

Promptotyping documents intervene in this problem as boundary objects in the sense of Star and Griesemer (1989), objects plastic enough to serve the needs of different communities and robust enough to maintain identity across them. A DATA document is readable by the scholar, who can verify that the description of the sources is right, and actionable for an agentic tool, which derives parsing and interface logic from it. A REQUIREMENTS document phrased as user stories is at once a scholarly statement about research practice and a specification. The method thereby relocates the site of interdisciplinary translation. What Kemman describes as negotiation in conversation between two people becomes a versioned, inspectable artefact; the trading zone acquires a written constitution. This is itself an epistemic claim about how interdisciplinary knowledge can be stabilised, and it holds independently of any generative tooling; a Promptotyping document set would improve communication with a human developer too. The generative setting merely makes the documents doubly load-bearing, because there the documents are not an aid to implementation but its source.

## 3. Promptotyping as a Method

### 3.1 Status and Provenance of the Method

Honesty about provenance first. Promptotyping was not designed on paper and then applied; it was distilled from practice. The documented projects of Section 5 were built across a period in which the method's vocabulary stabilised, and the earliest of them were conducted intuitively, with the formal document set emerging partly in retrospect. The analysis of my own project documentation shows the prescribed documents existing in fragments in the early cases and consolidating in the later ones [to verify against the case inventory]. The method defined below is therefore the consolidated form, the description of a practice at the point where it became teachable, not a protocol that every documented case followed from the start. I consider this the normal epistemology of method formation, comparable to how qualitative research methods are codified from exemplary studies, and state it so that the case typology of Section 5 can be read correctly; the cases validate the consolidated method to different degrees, and the later cases validate it more strictly than the early ones.

### 3.2 The Document Set and the Process

Promptotyping is iterative context engineering. Its unit of work is not code but a small set of versioned Markdown documents that together constitute the specification of the artefact, and its central loop iterates over these documents rather than over the implementation. The canonical set comprises:

- **README (context).** Project goals, scholarly context, intended users, constraints. The document a newcomer, human or machine, reads first.
- **DATA.** The structure, semantics, formats, and idiosyncrasies of the research data, including its gaps and uncertainties. Typically produced by compressing the results of exploratory data analysis into a compact description; from a sample of the actual JSON, XML, or CSV, iterations extract and condense the structure.
- **REQUIREMENTS.** Functional and non-functional requirements, represented as user stories of scholarly work with acceptance criteria; the direct continuation of scholar-centred design (Section 2.3).
- **DESIGN.** Interface and visualisation decisions with their reasons. This is where Drucker's critique becomes operational; conventions are not inherited but chosen and justified, or rejected.
- **INSTRUCTIONS.** The implementation directives handed to the agentic tool, including the technical guidelines of Section 4.
- **JOURNAL.** The record of iterations; what was tried, what failed, which model produced what, which decisions were taken and why. The journal is what makes the process citable and criticisable after the fact.

Not every project needs every document at full depth, and complex projects specialise the set [the complete, versioned document templates and rules are published in the companion vault; see Section 5.4]. What is constant is the double addressee. Every document must be readable by the scholar, because reading is how verification happens, and actionable by the agent, because the documents are the context from which the artefact is derived. This double function disciplines the writing; a data description too vague for a model to parse against is usually also too vague to count as scholarly documentation.

The process moves through four recurring phases. **Preparation** gathers the materials and articulates what the tool is for. **Exploration** probes the data and possible approaches without formal documentation; scripts, sketches, and throwaway prototypes clarify feasibility. **Distillation** compresses what exploration taught into the document set; this is the phase in which context engineering happens in the strict sense, the deliberate construction of the context a model will work from. **Implementation** hands the documents to an agentic coding tool, verifies the derived artefact against data and requirements, and feeds corrections back into the documents, not into the code. The loop from implementation back to distillation is the method's signature; when the artefact is wrong, the specification was wrong or incomplete, and it is the specification that is fixed. Iteration ends when the artefact satisfies the acceptance criteria of REQUIREMENTS and the verification checks of Section 6.2, not when the developer runs out of patience [abort criterion to be sharpened; open question 8].

**A worked example.** The mechanism is best seen in a single case followed through. The Aldersbach project [details to verify against project documentation] concerned late medieval monastic account books, structured financial records in XML of roughly 2.3 MB, data of exactly the kind my dissertation modelled. Preparation fixed the scholarly purpose; a historian should be able to browse the accounts, follow persons and commodities across entries, and see monetary values in comparable form despite the source's heterogeneous currencies. Exploration ran parsing experiments against the XML and revealed, among other things, the currency problem as the analytically decisive one. Distillation produced the document set; DATA described the record structure and explicitly flagged the currency heterogeneity and lacunae in the source, REQUIREMENTS phrased the historian's needs as user stories with acceptance criteria, and DESIGN specified, against the default convention, that converted monetary values must remain visibly marked as conversions with their rule stated, so that the interface does not lend false commensurability to the sources; a small, concrete instance of the Drucker mechanism of Section 2.2. Implementation handed these documents to an agentic coding tool, which derived a self-contained static web application; verification checked the rendered entries against the source XML by sampling, found conversion errors in an early iteration, and the correction went into DATA, where the conversion rule had been underspecified, after which the artefact was regenerated. The journal records the iterations, the model used, and the decisions [the full document set and a video record of the process are published in the companion; DOI placeholder].

The example shows the division of labour the method installs. The scholar models, specifies, and verifies; the agent implements. This division is defended here on normative rather than essentialist grounds. I do not claim that modelling, specifying, and verifying are things generative systems cannot do; I claim that they are the work that must remain with scholars because it carries scholarly responsibility, in the same sense in which authorship carries responsibility regardless of what tools contributed to a text.

### 3.3 Situating the Method in Agentic Engineering

The enabling condition of the implementation phase is the rapid maturation of agentic coding tools, systems that plan, execute multi-step changes across a codebase, run and inspect their own output, and iterate. The research literature has consolidated around this development (Liu et al. 2024 for the systematic survey of LLM-based agents in software engineering; the 2025 survey of AI agentic programming for the coding-agent focus). For Promptotyping, the shift from single-prompt generation to agentic workflows changes what the documents are. Against a single prompt, documents are helpful context; against an agent that works for minutes or hours without supervision, documents are the governing specification, the only stable instance of intent in a process whose intermediate states no one reads. Agentic engineering thus does not merely accelerate the method; it makes the document layer structurally necessary, and it moves the researcher decisively into the roles of specifier and verifier. Promptotyping can be read as the scholarly discipline of that role; where general agentic practice optimises for working software, Promptotyping optimises for a verifiable relation between a scholarly specification and a derived instrument.

### 3.4 Transitions towards Knowledge Engineering

The documents of a single project are the smallest case of something larger. In sustained practice they accumulate into a curated knowledge layer, in my case an Obsidian vault, in which data descriptions, design rules, technical guidelines, and case documentation are maintained as reusable, interlinked context. Two consequences follow. First, the marginal effort of a new promptotype falls as the layer grows, because DATA and DESIGN documents for related materials already half exist; the method compounds. Second, the layer is usable beyond tool-building. Curated project knowledge of this kind supports the simulation of research designs, ideation, and hypothesis formation, in which a model reasons against the accumulated context rather than against its training distribution alone. This transition, from context engineering for one artefact to knowledge engineering for a research practice, marks the method's open edge and connects it back to the premise of the introduction; the human work of modelling and curation is not what the method replaces but what it renders productive.

## 4. The Artefact Type. Static Exploratory Research Tools

The artefacts Promptotyping produces are, by deliberate default, self-contained static web tools; a set of HTML, CSS, and JavaScript files with the research data embedded or loaded from flat files, deployable on any static host and runnable from a local folder. Three properties motivate the default. Static artefacts are generable in one pass by current agentic tools, which keeps the derivation from documents to artefact inspectable. They are publishable without infrastructure, which matches the resource situation of the researchers the method addresses. And they are durable in exactly the sense the field's own sustainability discourse demands; minimal computing (Risam and Gil 2022) and the Endings Project have argued that the digital artefacts most likely to survive are those with the fewest moving parts, no server-side dependencies, and no build chains that rot.

The technical guidelines, published in full in the companion vault and condensed here, follow from these properties. Vanilla JavaScript is the default; frameworks are avoided because they age fast, resist inspection, and add nothing that exploratory instruments need. Where a problem is algorithmically deep, a single library may be vendored locally under a compromise rule with four criteria [the criteria as per the guidelines one-pager: the problem is genuinely beyond reasonable vanilla implementation; the library is self-contained and vendorable without a build step; its licence permits redistribution; its removal path is documented]. Heavy computation is precomputed; the artefact ships derived data, not pipelines. No external calls at runtime; the artefact must work offline, which is at once a longevity, a privacy, and a security property. And every artefact carries a provenance declaration stating that it was generated, from which documents, with which models and tools, and how it was verified; the artefact discloses its own conditions of production the way an edition discloses its editorial principles.

The limits of the format are equally part of its definition. Client-side processing bounds the data volume; experience across the documented cases places the comfortable range up to the order of ten thousand records [to verify], beyond which precomputation and sampling strategies must carry more of the load. Static artefacts do not support collaborative or server-mediated workflows, and persistence beyond the browser is out of scope. These are not deficiencies to be engineered away but the contour of the artefact type; where a project outgrows them, it has outgrown prototyping and entered software development, with everything that entails.

## 6. Discussion

### 6.1 Disposable Process, Durable Artefact

The sandcastle and the archive appear to pull in opposite directions; Section 2.2 embraced impermanence as an epistemic virtue, Section 4 built for longevity. The tension dissolves once process and publication are distinguished. Within the research process, promptotypes are disposable in exactly Hinrichs' sense; iterations are cheap to discard, regeneration from amended documents is the normal case, and the value of a failed iteration is what it taught the document set. The artefact that a project publishes, by contrast, enters the scholarly record and inherits its obligations; hence the Endings-compliant format, the provenance declaration, and the versioned documents from which it can be understood and criticised. Disposability governs the making, durability the made. The documents are what connects the two, because they persist across discarded iterations and accompany the published artefact as its specification.

### 6.2 Verification

The obvious objection to generated research instruments is quality, and the answer developed across this paper is verification anchored in the documents. Concretely, verification in the documented cases operates at three levels [practice to be systematised; open question 9]. Data fidelity is checked by sampling the artefact's renderings against the source data. Requirement satisfaction is checked against the acceptance criteria of REQUIREMENTS, which is what user stories with acceptance criteria are for. Design conformance is checked by reading the artefact against DESIGN, which is where tool criticism (2.1) becomes a routine act rather than an occasional genre; the reviewer of a promptotype has, for once, the tool's explicit assumptions in hand. What the method cannot do is make verification automatic, and it should not pretend to; verification is precisely the scholarly labour the division of responsibility of 3.2 assigns to the human, and its cost is the honest price of the possibility claim.

### 6.3 Limits

The limits are of four kinds. Data volume bounds the artefact type (Section 4). Persistence and collaboration lie outside it. Model dependence is real; regenerating an artefact from identical documents with a different model, or the same model later, yields a different implementation, so reproducibility attaches to the documents and the process record, not to the binary identity of artefacts, and the provenance declaration exists to make this explicit. And the convention problem of 5.3 remains only partially solved; DESIGN documents can override model defaults case by case, but the gravitational pull of learned conventions is constant, and a method that requires vigilance has not eliminated a problem, only made it addressable. To these I add the evidential limit already declared; the validation offered here is a typology of documented cases with inspectable process records, not a controlled evaluation, and claims about what the method achieves for others, especially non-hybrid scholars, rest most heavily on the teaching cases of Type E.

### 6.4 Transferability beyond the Humanities

The paper has argued from the humanities and claimed generality; the claim can now be stated precisely. Nothing in the method's mechanics is humanities-specific; documents as boundary objects, the specifier-verifier role, the static artefact type, and the verification levels of 6.2 apply wherever structured research data meets a scholar without development capacity. What is humanities-specific is the difficulty profile the method was forged against, heterogeneous formats, interpretative modelling, semantic density, uncertainty as a first-class property of the data. Fields whose data is schema-regular and whose conventions of representation are settled face a strictly easier version of the problem. The transfer is therefore an inference in the favourable direction, from the hard case to easier ones, and I offer it as such; testing it is work for practitioners of those fields, and the companion's templates are built to be taken.

## 7. Conclusion

Promptotyping is a methodological answer to a gap the digital humanities have documented for twenty years, between scholars who model research data and the interactive instruments through which that data could be explored. The method's two claims are that versioned documents can carry the derivation of exploratory tools, concentrating scholarly work where scholarly responsibility lies, in modelling, specifying, and verifying; and that the same documents function as boundary objects that stabilise interdisciplinary translation in an inspectable form. The artefacts are exploratory research instruments, disposable in the making and durable in publication, and the technical guidelines anchored in the document layer are the condition under which generated artefacts qualify as verifiable instruments of research. This paper is the method's canonical definition; the versioned companion carries its full evidence and its working materials, and the invitation of the final section is literal. The method was distilled from practice, and practice, in the humanities and beyond, is where it will be tested.
