# Abstract

Promptotyping is an iterative context-engineering method for producing data-driven research artifacts with frontier large language models. Across four phases (Preparation, Exploration and Mapping, Distillation, Implementation) it treats structured context documents, the Promptotyping Documents, as the primary research artifact, while the generated prototype is a functional by-product that can be discarded and regenerated from them. The paper formalises the method and traces its genealogy to the author's doctoral research on modelling historical information, where Scholar-Centred Design turned user stories into structured requirements for research interfaces; frontier models then made the mapping from research data and structured context onto artifacts executable by the model itself. A standardisation stage extends this document practice into a metadata practice, with a typology of documents (Knowledge, Process, Action), a reduced frontmatter core, and a resolvable template address that declares a repository's method. Evidence comes from 20 projects in the Digital Humanities and adjacent fields (2023 to early 2026), ranging from a two-hour annotation experiment to a multi-week OCR/TEI pipeline; the quantitative claims of the evidence section were adversarially verified against the public repositories, a self-application of the method's own verification practice. From this practice the paper develops the concept of epistemic infrastructure and a typology of research data interfaces classified by epistemic function (Verification, Exploration, Edition, Capture, Audit). The method presupposes a Critical Expert in the Loop; its evidence base is a single practitioner's documented projects, and the discussion names the resulting limits, among them the missing control condition and the confounding of method effects with model capability.
