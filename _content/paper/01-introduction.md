# 1. Introduction

The relationship between conceptual work and implementation in research tool development has inverted. Writing code for interfaces and pipelines was the time-consuming part; today, with Frontier LLMs capable of generating executable code from natural language specifications, it is fast. What was previously optional — formalising requirements, explicating domain knowledge, building validation structures — is now the decisive factor that determines whether LLM-generated outputs are useful or unreliable (Mei et al. 2025).

This shift did not happen gradually. A convergence of advances in pre-training scale, post-training alignment, and agentic tool use (Section 2.7) crossed a practical threshold in 2025: LLM-based coding agents became capable of navigating repositories, executing pipelines, running tests, and iterating on their own outputs. For fields like the Digital Humanities, where research is computer-based and data follows established semantic standards (TEI-XML, RDF, JSON-LD), the consequence is that a single domain expert with methodological literacy can now produce research tools that previously required dedicated development teams.

This observation emerged from sustained practice. During doctoral research on interactive visualisations for historical financial sources (Pollin 2025b), the author built a monolithic dashboard for exploring medieval accounting data — a system designed to serve social historians asking "Who worked for whom?" and economic historians asking "How did grain prices develop?" through a single interface. By the time the dissertation was completed, the approach had become obsolete. Not visualisation itself, but the assumption that heterogeneous research questions could be served by a single, static interface. LLMs had made it feasible to generate purpose-built, question-specific interfaces instead — interfaces tailored to one historian's user story rather than compromising across many.

What remained valuable — and became more important — was not the code but the structured context that had been developed to build the dashboard: Scholar-Centred Design workshops with historians, user stories derived from different source types, data models formalised in ontologies, annotation guidelines for TEI-XML encoding. This structured context turned out to be precisely what an LLM needs to generate contextually appropriate research artifacts. The monolithic dashboard was bound to a single output type; the knowledge behind it was the actual artifact — and that knowledge is not bound to any particular output. The same structured context can produce an interface, a pipeline, an ontology, or a data model, depending on the research question. The Bookkeeping Ontology developed during the dissertation (Pollin 2025b) illustrates this: it emerged through exactly the kind of structured domain knowledge elicitation that Promptotyping now systematises, and its production process would today be promptotypable.

From this experience, a method crystallised that we call _Promptotyping_ — though it draws on two distinct origins. The dissertation supplied the insight that structured context, rather than code, is the durable research artifact; a parallel line of prompt-framework practice, developed in dialogue with the models themselves, supplied the insight into the dialogic, documented process by which such artifacts are made (Section 2.6). It is an iterative context engineering method in four phases for building data-driven research artifacts with Frontier LLMs, documented in an earlier blog publication (Pollin 2025d) and developed further through over a dozen projects since. This paper formalises the method, reports on its application across projects of increasing complexity, and introduces two conceptual extensions — _epistemic infrastructure_ and a theoretically grounded _typology of research data interfaces_ classified by epistemic function — that emerged from this practice.

The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 defines key terms, positions Promptotyping relative to adjacent practices, traces its genealogy, and describes the technical trajectory that makes the method viable. Section 3 describes the four phases and introduces a typology of Promptotyping Documents. Section 4 presents projects that demonstrate the method at different scales. Section 5 develops the concept of epistemic infrastructure and proposes a typology of Promptotyping Interfaces classified by epistemic function. Section 6 discusses implications — including the role of the Critical Expert, asymmetric amplification, and the anticipated impact on Digital Humanities research — and limitations.
