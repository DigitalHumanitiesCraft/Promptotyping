# 7. Conclusion

Promptotyping formalises a practice that emerged from sustained work with Frontier LLMs across diverse Digital Humanities projects. The method's four phases (Preparation, Exploration and Mapping, Distillation, Implementation) provide a structured alternative to Vibe Coding that preserves the speed of LLM-assisted development while introducing the documentation and verification practices necessary for scholarly accountability.

Four contributions extend the initial method description (Pollin 2025d). The typology of Promptotyping Documents (Knowledge, Process, Action) provides an analytical framework for the diverse Markdown-based working documents that accumulate in LLM-assisted projects, and the vault model developed in Section 3.3 operationalises this typology through three structural commitments — function before filename, inclusion by trigger rather than checklist, and diagnostic decoupling between function and type — that answer the practical question of which documents a project needs without prescribing a fixed catalogue, with the `knowledge/` folder understood as a simplified research vault that ports the established Markdown-and-wikilink practice of personal knowledge work into an agentic repository. The concept of epistemic infrastructure identifies the integrated system of components — Verification Milestones, Promptotyping Interfaces, living documents, version control, and agentic coding — that emerges when the method is applied to complex, multi-stage pipelines. The typology of Promptotyping Interfaces, classifying over 20 browser-based tools into five categories by epistemic function (Verification, Exploration, Edition, Capture, Audit), addresses a gap across HCI, visual analytics, and digital humanities classification frameworks — none of which uses epistemic function as the primary organising principle for research data interfaces. The distinction between single-agent mode (_Promptotyping a Tool_) and multi-agent mode (_Promptotyping a Research Project_), introduced in Section 3.5, generalises the method to projects with the structure of a research organisation, where a Research Lead Agent coordinates Sub-Agents whose roles, permissions, and knowledge zones are themselves objects of methodological design.

The method is not bound to a specific output type. Interfaces, pipelines, ontologies, data models, and annotation schemas are different manifestations of the same context transformation mechanism. The Bookkeeping Ontology, built manually through Scholar-Centred Design, and the M³GIM data model, iteratively refined through Promptotyping, illustrate this mechanism in two different methodological paradigms — separated by the threshold described in Section 2.7, but connected by the same principle: research artifacts emerge from structured context knowledge, and their quality depends on the quality of the context, not the code.

The progression documented across 20 projects — from a two-hour annotation tool to a pipeline processing 4,150 pages, from a single structured prompt to repositories with around thirty knowledge documents — demonstrates that the method scales with project complexity and with LLM capability improvements. This is not automation: it is advanced context engineering by domain experts who bring the knowledge that the model lacks and the judgement that the model cannot provide. A consequence of this infrastructure is asymmetric amplification — the method disproportionately benefits researchers who combine domain expertise with methodological literacy, rather than substituting for either.

The method evolves with every project and with every new model generation. What is described here is the state of practice as of early 2026. What will persist, we believe, are the principles: structured context over ad hoc prompting, verification over trust, documentation over speed, and expert judgement as the irreducible core.

Several questions remain open. Can Promptotyping Documents develop into a metadata standard that makes LLM-assisted research processes documentable and citable? The three structural commitments, the reduced frontmatter core, and the versioned, publicly published template catalogue described in Section 3.3 are a first step in this direction — they name a repository as a Promptotyping vault, declare its method through a resolvable `template:` address, and expose its external knowledge anchors as URI mappings — but a community-validated standard would need to emerge from broader practice, not from a single author's specification. How can research integrity be ensured when central tools are proprietary black boxes — and what does reproducibility mean when the generating process is stochastic? Who can afford this research practice, given that subscription costs and infrastructure requirements create new inequalities? Does shared reliance on the same Frontier models introduce structural biases into research outputs — and if so, is this epistemic homogenisation or merely a new form of methodological dependence? Does the multi-agent mode introduced in Section 3.5 redistribute cognitive load productively, or does the design of the agent organisation itself become the new bottleneck? And finally: as the models continue to improve along the trajectory described in Section 2.7, does the method scale toward partial automation of research — or does the Critical Expert's role become more important, not less, as the stakes of each decision increase? These questions cannot be answered from within the method. They require empirical study, institutional deliberation, and a willingness to engage critically with technologies whose consequences are not yet fully visible.
