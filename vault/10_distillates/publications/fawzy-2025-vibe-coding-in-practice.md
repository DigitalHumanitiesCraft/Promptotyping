# Distillate: Vibe Coding in Practice (Fawzy et al. 2025)

A systematic grey-literature review of practitioner accounts of vibe coding; the paper cites it in Section 2.5 for the empirical finding that vibe coding is fast but perceived as unreliable, the speed-quality trade-off it names the paradox.

## Core statements

- The review found a speed-quality trade-off paradox in which practitioners are drawn to speed and accessibility yet judge the resulting code as fast but flawed. ^s1
  > "Our analysis reveals a speed–quality trade-off paradox, where vibe coders are motivated by speed and accessibility, often experiencing rapid "instant success and flow", yet most perceive the resulting code as fast but flawed." (fawzy-2025, Abstract)
- The review's overall verdict is that vibe coding lowers barriers and speeds prototyping at a cost to reliability and maintainability. ^s2
  > "vibe coding lowers barriers and accelerates prototyping, but at the cost of reliability and maintainability." (fawzy-2025, Abstract)
- Across the practitioner corpus, motivation by speed combined with minimal review produced fragile or error-prone code. ^s3
  > "We find that users are primarily motivated by speed and accessibility, but their reliance on minimal review often results in fragile or error-prone code." (fawzy-2025, §1)
- The evidence base is a grey-literature search that retrieved 154 practitioner sources and, after quality and inclusion filtering, analysed the 101 that remained. ^s4
  > "Our search string retrieved 154 grey literature sources (e.g., blog posts, online articles, and technical reports) ... All other 53 sources were excluded." (fawzy-2025, §4)

## Terms

- **Speed-quality trade-off paradox**: the review's name for the pattern that vibe coders value rapid output while simultaneously distrusting the reliability of what they produce. [[10_distillates/publications/fawzy-2025-vibe-coding-in-practice#^s1]]

## Open questions

- The corpus is self-reported practitioner grey literature, not controlled measurement, so the reliability judgment is a perception rather than a defect count.
